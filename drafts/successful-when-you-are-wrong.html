    Successful When You Are Wrong

    The curious cases when the wrong theories can be useful

    This piece is a meditation, an exploration, on how we have fields like medicine, psychology, advertising /propaganda which were successful in their practice even though their theories are later known to be wrong. About how wrong theories have kernels of truth in them, but the explanation narrative was wrong.

    As a child, I heard how Christopher Columbus had problems getting funding because he knew that the world was round, but the European kings thought that it was flat. Columbus persisted until the Catholic queen Isabella was convinced, funded him, so Columbus sailed off to find a Western route to the Indies. This is a standard, beautiful story on how an intellectual rebel stood against society until he proved them wrong.

    Except that the story is a lie. Europeans knew that the world was a sphere. They also had a good idea on how big the world was. They correctly rejected Columbus' wrong Earth circumferance calculations. Geographers of the time knew that India and the East Indies were a lot farther away. Columbus planning was wrong. Columbus was lucky that there was an unknown continent right about the place where he was going to run out of provisions. For many years he thought he reached the Philippines. It is still unclear if he died thinking so, or if he eventually realized that he reached another continent.

    The Columbus story is not the one about a daring intellectual maverik and his enterprising royal backer, but of two dummies that got lucky. If the Americas weren't in the middle of the ocean, all of those people would have died. It was a reckless, dangerous plan that put the lives of his crew in danger. Why did Isabella fund the voyage? Perhaps she desperately wanted to catch up to the Portuguese's success in Africa. Maybe expelling the Muslims and the Jews left her without the scholars who could have exposed Columbus for the crackpot that he was.

    Columbus' theory was wrong, but not entirely wrong. He did think the world was a sphere. His intuition was right: there was a Western route to the Indies. He foolishly dismissed experts' opinions on the size of the earth. He executed a plan based on his wrong theory, and he succeded, mostly by luck. And this is the core of being right when you wrong: your theory is wrong, but has kernels of truth. When we act on the wrong theories, we are successful, but mostly by luck. The people who succeed with the wrong ideas often refused to believe that their model was incorrect. After all, they succeeded.

    I find this fascinating. How we can be wrong, but still be right? Let me restate. We can create the wrong model of the world, act based on that wrong model, yet still excute our plans successfully.

    This runs counter to how we are taught how science should work. The common narrative is that we create a hypotheses, we test it, and then we get to accept or reject it. A wrong model should be rejected. Over time, we build up on the correct theories, so our understanding of the world becomes greater and greater. Europeans have been using the scientific method for the last 500 years or so, so we live during a period with the best understanding of our physical and social universe.

    Once we try to practice that scientific method, though, we discover that things are not so clear-cut. Our experiments return mixed results. Our measurements are not exact enough. The data supports our theory when using the right statistical tool, but rejects if we use the another. We don't know what to do with the outliers. Our experiment indicates that we are wrong, but we know in our heart that we are right, so maybe we ran the experiment incorrectly?

    The cloud of uncertainty around the scientific method has been known for a while. Thomas S. Kuhn wrote a famous and influential book on the subject, "The Structure of Scientific Revolutions." The book gives us another model, another narrative, on how the practice of science works. Kuhn says that we go through periods where we do accumulate scientific knowledge by using the collection of accepted theories, which Kuhn calls paradigms. At some point the limitations of those theories become too much to ignore, and that triggers the search for alternatives. Once a good alternative is found and adopted, we witness a paradigm shift. This paradigm shift doesn't have the practitioners of the old paradigm accept the validity of the new paradigm. Usually they stick to the old one, since they have had a lifetime of success with it. Instead, the paradigm takes a hold when the old guard dies off.

    Reading Kuhn's description of scientific progress sounds more correct than the never-ending progress narrative which we still teach to students. It sounds like a straight description of what happens, similar to describing how rain falls from the sky. In one of those delightful example of recursion in real life, Kuhn's book was viciously attacked by the practitioners of the old paradigm on how scientific progress happens. By now, the bulk of those old practitioners have retired or died.

    I will admit right now that my description of Kuhn's work is what I was taught by my professors in university and what I learned by reading the wikipedia article on his work. It is the meme version. I want to make it clear because many of my intellectual heroes commited the minor sin of pretending to have read works which they hadn't. They relied on the meme version, to appear more well-read than they were. I find it cute since some, like Octavio Paz, actually were well-read, perhaps had read more books than most of the people he interacted with, but still would indulge in this teenage deception, now and then. I only learned that he had not read some book when I finally read the source myself to discover that Paz's description of the book had nothing to do with the contents. It is not about condemning Paz in some sort of neo-puritanical purging energy that have been so popular in the last 10 years. Instead, I share this because it is amusing and humanizing. We see how the leading intellectual of Mexico, who had a huge amount of cultural power, who could create or destroy careers, who won a Nobel literature prize, he still had the same intellectual insecurities that we all have, or being afraid that people would think less of us if we admitted that we had not read a book yet.

    I am still going to use the popular version of Kuhn's ideas as the basic explanation on how we move from one model of reality to another. What I want focus on is, how were the discarded models thought to be true in the first place?

    The foundation for European medicine is physic, the theory of the humors. This is where our word for a medicna doctor, "physician", comes from. Briefly, the idea is that our bodies contain four humors, blood, yellow bile, black bile, and phlegm. Illness happens when there the balance between these four humor is broken. To heal people, we try to bring the humors back into balance. This was achieved by a numbers of practices. If one had excessive blood, then the treatment was to bleed the patient until the balance was restored.

    This was one of the most powerful medical theories in Europe for centuries. The theory existed in Mesopotamia, made its way to the Meditarranean, where Greek and then Roman doctors adopted. It is important to stress that, although dominant, it wasn't the only way that Europeans practiced medicine. There were traditional folk healers, most likely women, who used herbs. After the spread of Islam to Spain, there were also Muslim doctors, who relied on a collection of knowledge of what treatments worked for what conditions. Europe also saw the rise of a series of healers such as homeopathy and other collection of practices that survive to this day.

    The physic practitioners saw themselves as men of science, as opposed to the folky, ignorant, or outright incorrect that their competitors were practicing medicine.Unlike the village auntie healer, they understood the principles of health of our bodies. Using those principles, they could deduce the correct treatment. This high opinion of themseves wasn't shared with the greater population. Reading literature or history of the time, one finds famous people, like kings, being suspicious of physicians, claiming that they brought death wherever they showed up. This unfortunate track record was founded in reality since a common treatment when one had an excess of blood was bleeding the patient. An aggressive treatment may have had an aggressive outcome.

    I haven't seen any statistics, but from historic accounts, they appeared to have a success rate similar to the other healers. This success rate makes sense because they were practicing medicine at a time when world science didn't understand how our bodies worked or where illnesses come from.
