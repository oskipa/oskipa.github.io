    Descartes Escape Room

    How Descartes creates a perception escape room which he wasn't able to leave

    Descartes is a wonderful writer. He writes in a straightforward way, with as little jargon as possible. I read Descartes as a child after I bought an abridged copy of the "Discourse on the Method." I vainly thought that meant that I was preciously smart. What it meant is that Descartes wrote so clearly that a fith grader can read Discourse and feel successful at it.

    I reread Discourse as an adult, the unabridged version. The book is still fascinating. It is still clear and understandable, but now my greater knowledge of philosophy, history, science, and the necesity to earn a living made me appreciate it more. If you are interested in Descartes, Discourse is the book that you should read. It is the summary of his most important investigations, written by Descartes himself. Descarte talks about his method, but also about some of the discoveries that he made with this method with metaphysics, proving the existance of God, blood circulation, and some others.

    I recall my surprise when I reach the end of Discourse, and recognized a sales pitch. Discourse was a sales catalogue. Descartes is seeking funding to do more research. Discourse is a sampling of his interests, of his thoughts, his investigations. If you liked what you see, imagine what else he could do if you funded him so that he can work exclusively on research. It is so humbling to see that the founder of modern European philosophy also had to seek grants. He eventually got one, in Sweden, whose harsher weather may have led to his death.

    Descartes is famous for his "Cogito ergo sum," "I think, therefore, I am." If after reading his summary in Discourse you decide to go deeper, you should look into "Meditations on First Philosophy." "Meditations" is one of the works where is goes more in depth on the subject.
